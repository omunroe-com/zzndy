BONUS & OTHER PAYMENTS

Article 28.1 of the 2001 Model Contract provides for the payment of a signature bonus. The amount of the signature bonus is negotiable but amounts up to US$3 million are indicative in contracts signed to date and this amount has been assumed here. There is no provision in the 2001 Model Contract for the payment of a discovery bonus and none has been assumed here.

Article 28.2 of the 2001 Model Contract provides for the payment of production bonuses upon start of commercial production and at two unspecified daily production thresholds. The amounts of the production bonuses are negotiable and can vary a great deal with payments at any of the thresholds reaching several million US dollars. The following production thresholds and bonuses have been assumed here:

Average Daily Production Production Bonus (US$MM)
Oil (MBOPD) (1) Gas (MMCFD) (2)
At production start-up 1.0

Notes:
1) Production thresholds as provided in the 1998 Model Contract;
2) Gas production bonuses assumed on an equivalence basis using 6,000 cubic feet of gas = 1 barrel of oil.

Bonuses relate to the entire contract area and are non-recoverable costs.

Article 21.7 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that the contractor must make annual contributions to the Hydrocarbons Support Fund (Fonds de Soutien des Hydrocarbures) created to develop petroleum research activities in Gabon. Under the 2001 Model Contract, two payments are due:

  • a negotiable annual contribution in US dollars during exploration and production periods, which contribution is cost recoverable (annual contributions of US$50,000 during exploration period and US$100,000 during production period have been assumed here); and
  • US$0.05 per barrel of available production (gross production in the contract area less losses and use in operations) during each year of the production period, which contribution is not cost recoverable.

Notes:

  1. For gas production, the second Hydrocarbons Support Fund payment has been assumed at US$0.008/MCF.

  2. Under the 1998 Model Contract, the following two payments were due:

    • an annual contribution of US$350,000 (although understood to be negotiable) during the entire contract, and recoverable for cost recovery purposes; and

    • a fee of US$0.05 per barrel of available production (gross production in the contract area less losses and use in operations), and not recoverable for cost recovery purposes.

  3. Some actual contracts include a contribution towards the equipment of the Direction Generale des Hydrocarbures (DGH) or one-time payments in addition to the annual Hydrocarbons Support Fund payments.

Article 39.1 of the 2001 Model Contract provides for the payment of a negotiable annual training fee. The 2001 Model Contract requires one rate to be fixed for the exploration period and another for the development and production periods. The amounts agreed in contracts signed to date vary - indicative amounts are US$50,000 rising to US$200,000 per year depending on the phase of the development. We have assumed US$50,000 per year during exploration phase and US$150,000 per year during the production phase. Article 39.2 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that the training fees are recoverable costs.

STATE PARTICIPATION

Under a production sharing contract the state may assume a participating interest in the contract at a level agreed at the time of award and exercisable at the time of commercial development. The level of the state's participation interest is negotiable but in practice the rate has varied between 5% and 25%, with an overall decreasing trend over the past 20 years or so (5% has been assumed here). The state's participating interest is carried through to production start-up. Carried development costs are reimbursed, with interest (assumed to be 8%), from 100% of state's share of revenue. Carried exploration costs are not reimbursed. (2001 MC Art 19.3)

Notes:

  1. The 2001 Model Contract limits annual reimbursement to 70% of the state's share of revenue. However, this provision appears to be negotiable.

  2. The 1998 Model Contract set an upper limit of 10% and the 1993 Model Contract of 25% for state participation. The 1998 Model Contract also reserved an additional 5% for Gabonese private investors. If the Gabonese investors did not take up this option, it was made available to the state.

ROYALTY

Article 26.1 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that royalty shall be levied on gross production less any volumes lost, used in operations, or re-injected into the reservoir. The 2001 Model Contract stipulates the payment of royalty at an unspecified fixed flat rate (5% has been assumed here).

Notes:

  1. Royalty rates have always been a negotiable item and, as a result, have varied considerably. Even in the 1980s when most contracts included flat-rate royalties, these could vary between 5% and 20% from contract to contract. The 1993 Model Contract specified a flat rate royalty of 20% for crude oil and 16% for gas. Later, during the 1990s, sliding scale rates were adopted in most contracts with the 1998 Model Contract providing for royalty to be payable at variable rates linked to a sliding scale of production.
  2. In launching the eighth round for "ultra-deep" water areas in 1997, the government announced that it would agree to royalty rates lower than those agreed to previously and this has been borne out in deep water awards to date.

COST RECOVERY

Article 24.2 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that recoverable costs are expensed and recovered immediately from gross revenue less royalty subject to a negotiable ceiling. The negotiable ceiling is indicatively in the range 75% to 80% (80% has been assumed here) of gross revenue less royalty. Un-recovered costs may be carried forward indefinitely until fully recovered but not beyond the duration of the contract.

Note: In the 1998 Model Contract, a ceiling of 55% of net production was specified, which could be increased to 75% if, after the first five years of production, the contractor had yet to recover all of its "investment in development". In practice, the cost recovery ceiling is one of the key negotiable terms of the contract and a variety of arrangements have been agreed in the past. Indeed, the government emphasised in 1997 that for "ultra-deep" water blocks offered in the eighth round the ceiling would be adjusted to speed up cost recovery and this appears to be borne out by recent awards. Some contracts are understood to have featured various ceilings related to the water depth in which a field is located and at least one contract makes provision for an uplift of recoverable costs (i.e. more than 100% of actual costs incurred).

PROFIT SHARING

Article 25 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that production remaining after royalty and cost recovery is shared between the contractor and the state at negotiable rates linked to an incremental sliding scale of average daily production levels. The 2001 Model Contract indicates four production tranches with both the tranche levels and profit shares left open for negotiation. The state's share of production includes a portion equivalent to the amount of corporation tax which the contractor would otherwise be liable to pay. The following profit shares have been assumed here:

Note: In practice, profit sharing is one of the key negotiable terms of the contract and a variety of arrangements have been agreed in the past. Sliding scales are usually used with the number of tranches ranging from two to six. The government has mentioned the possibility of linking profit shares to measures of profitability instead of production, although this approach does not appear to have been adopted in actual contracts.

INCOME TAX

Article 26 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that the contractor is subject to corporation tax on income, payment of which is made in kind by delivery to the state of a quantity of petroleum corresponding to the amount of said tax. The state's share of production under the profit sharing provisions includes the part representing the income tax due by the contractor. Thus, the contractor's income tax liability is discharged by the state on its behalf.

Note: The tax rate applicable to remaining concession contracts is 73% while the generally applicable income tax rate is 35%.

DOMESTIC SUPPLY OBLIGATION

Article 35 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that investors may be required to supply local refineries with a proportion of production (equal to the contract production as a proportion of total Gabonese production) at a discounted price. In practice, the discount may be negotiable typically ranging 10% - 20%.

The discount is a recoverable cost. This provision effectively has a zero net impact on the contractor's cash flow. The contractor is obliged to sell a proportion of its cost oil and profit oil at a discount but the value of the discount is recoverable. Thus, apart from the lag of recovering the discount (one year), the contractor does not lose any value. As a result, this obligation has not been modelled.

Note: The 1998 model contract specified a discount of 25%.

DIVIDEND WITHHOLDING TAX

Article 26.7 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that the companies comprising the contractor are exempt from any tax and withholding at source on the distribution of profits from operations under a PSC.

Note: Generally, dividends paid are subject to a 20% withholding tax.

RING FENCE

Around the contract area for royalty, cost recovery, profit sharing, and income tax.

Quality

Gabonese authorities issue model PSCs from time to time. The most recent model contract was issued in 2001, replacing an earlier 1998 version issued in conjunction with the eighth round. The 2001 model contract is understood to form the foundation of negotiations for all new awards and its provisions are used as a basis of this model. All terms are negotiable. Royalty rates are IHS assumption based on higher rates from the sliding scale used in 1998 Model Contract; bonuses, annual contributions, cost recovery ceiling, and profit sharing rates and trigger points assumed in this model are indicative of contracts signed for oil in ultra deep water areas (> 1,000m water depth) in late 1990s - early 2000s.

Article 30 of the 2001 Model Contract provides that in the event of a commercial gas discovery the state guarantees to grant to the contractor fiscal terms which are at least as favourable as terms granted to other oil companies operating in Gabon with all other provisions of the contract applying mutatis mutandis. For the purposes of calculation of bonuses, cost recovery, and profit sharing, gas equivalents are to be calculated using 165 m3 of gas = 1 barrel of oil. In the current model we have assumed gas calculated on an equivalence basis using 6,000 cubic feet of gas = 1 barrel of oil.
